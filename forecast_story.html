Why This Report

Food Prices Are Rising — But Not Equally

Over the past decade, prices for staple foods in Nigeria have risen between 153% and 746%. Understanding where prices are heading helps families, traders, and policymakers make smarter decisions.

For Households

Know which foods will surge so you can stock up in cheaper months (typically January). A ₦5,000 saving per week adds up to ₦260,000 a year — enough to pay school fees.

For Traders & Businesses

Spot seasonal buy-low windows and price spikes before they happen. Importing rice or sourcing beans at the right time can mean the difference between profit and loss.

For Policymakers

Identify which food corridors are most volatile and which states suffer most. Targeted buffer stock programmes and subsidy timing can be evidence-driven rather than reactive.

Meet the AI Models

Two Different Brains,
One Question: What Comes Next?

We used two well-tested forecasting methods. Each has its own style of "thinking". Understanding the difference helps you trust — or question — the numbers.

Model 01 · Meta

Prophet

The Calendar-Aware Analyst

"Think of Prophet as a highly experienced market analyst who has worked for 10 years and learned every seasonal pattern. He knows that beans always spike in October because of demand before the dry season, and that prices dip in January after the harvest. He reads long-term trends and seasonal rhythms with calm confidence."

Prophet is a statistical model developed by Meta (Facebook). It decomposes price history into three layers: the long-term trend (are prices generally rising?), yearly seasonality (how do months compare to each other?), and special events. It then projects these patterns forward in time.

  • Excellent at capturing seasonal cycles (e.g., harvest rhythms)
  • Provides confidence bands — a range of where the price might land
  • Easy to interpret and explain to non-technical audiences
  • Struggles when markets behave unpredictably — average error of 24.9%
  • Does not learn from fine-grained price signals across different items
Model 02 · Microsoft

LightGBM

The Pattern-Recognition Machine

"Think of LightGBM as a highly precise data detective. It doesn't rely on calendar intuition. Instead, it reads thousands of tiny clues — the month, last month's price, the 3-month trend, whether it's dry season — and finds the hidden combinations that predict tomorrow's price. It's less of a storyteller, more of a calculator."

LightGBM is a gradient-boosted decision tree model made by Microsoft. It learns by making thousands of small predictions and correcting its own mistakes step by step. It treats forecasting like a puzzle, piecing together dozens of input signals — called features — to arrive at a price estimate.

  • Best-in-class accuracy — average error of only 7.6%
  • Learns from complex, non-linear relationships in the data
  • Adapts faster to recent price shocks and market events
  • Does not produce a built-in confidence band (less transparent uncertainty)
  • Needs more data engineering; less intuitive to explain step-by-step

Accuracy Face-Off

Which Model was Right More Often?

We tested both models against 12 months of real price data they had never seen. Lower error = closer to the actual price. Think of it as a prediction contest judged on past performance.

3.3×

LightGBM is 3.3× More Accurate Than Prophet

Across all 44 food items, Prophet's average prediction error was 24.9% — meaning if rice costs ₦1,000, its forecast could be off by ₦249. LightGBM's average error was just 7.6% — off by only ₦76 on the same item. That's the difference between a useful forecast and an unreliable one.

Important Note on Model Disagreement

Prophet and LightGBM often give different forecasts for the same item. Prophet tends to show higher prices for eggs, yam, and rice because it projects long-term upward trends. LightGBM, being more sensitive to recent data, sometimes shows stabilisation after an initial spike. When the two models agree, you can be more confident. When they disagree significantly, treat the forecast range as wide.

What Should You Do With This?

Data without action is just numbers. Here are concrete steps for three different audiences, based directly on the forecast findings.

For Households

Smart Family Shopping

  • 1
    Buy beans and eggs in January–March when prices hit their yearly low. Stock up with 2–3 months' supply if you have storage.
  • 2
    Expect to spend more on fish by mid-2026. Dried sardine and catfish are forecast to rise 70–83%. Consider building a small reserve now.
  • 3
    Plantain and sweet potato show modest rises. These remain relatively affordable staples — consider increasing their share in your household diet.
  • 4
    Avoid bulk tomato purchases in May–June. Tomato prices historically spike then, driven by mid-year rainfall disruption to supply chains.
For Traders & Businesses

Strategic Sourcing Windows

  • 1
    Import rice in Q1 (Jan–Mar). Forecasts show significant price rises for imported high-quality rice through 2026. Source early before demand outpaces supply.
  • 2
    Watch the egg market closely. At +127% LightGBM forecast, agric eggs represent one of the biggest arbitrage opportunities — but also the highest risk from supply shocks.
  • 3
    Use LightGBM's forecast as your primary price baseline. With 3× lower error vs Prophet, it consistently gives a more reliable price estimate for operational planning.
  • 4
    Groundnut oil peaks in December. Build inventory in January and ride the year-long price trend — forecast shows a rise from ₦3,093 to ₦4,172 (LGB) by Dec 2026.
For Policymakers

Evidence-Based Interventions

  • 1
    Prioritise protein stabilisation. Eggs, catfish, and dried fish show the highest forecast volatility. Targeted subsidies or cold chain infrastructure investments would have maximum impact.
  • 2
    Beans and maize require harvest-period intervention. Both staples spike in Oct–Dec. Pre-positioning buffer stocks by September could dampen the seasonal shock.
  • 3
    The geographic price gap is large. In most items, the most expensive state pays 3–5× more than the cheapest. Transport infrastructure and market information systems can reduce this gap.
  • 4
    Deploy monthly price monitoring via LightGBM. Its 7.6% average error is accurate enough to serve as an early warning system for imminent price shocks — update forecasts monthly with fresh data.
Methodology

How This Analysis Was Built

The dataset covers 44 food commodities tracked monthly across Nigeria's 36 states and the FCT from January 2016 through December 2024 — totalling over 180,000 price records from the National Bureau of Statistics.

Each model was trained on data from 2016 to 2023, then tested on 2024 (12 months of unseen data). The MAPE scores shown in this report reflect that out-of-sample accuracy test. Forecasts extend from January 2025 to December 2026.

For Prophet, each item was modelled independently with yearly seasonality and country-specific holidays. For LightGBM, features included lagged prices (1, 3, 6, 12 months), rolling averages, month-of-year, and year indicators — trained as a unified cross-item regression model.

All figures are in Nigerian Naira (₦). Prices represent national averages. State-level forecasts are available in the supplementary data files.
